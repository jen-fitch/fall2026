Jenkins Hall

The renovated Jenkins Hall gives Sculpture and Ceramics a modern home while honoring 75 years of hands-on learning.

Cal Poly Humboldt · Fall 2026

1950

Industrial Arts Roots

75 years of hands-on learning in Jenkins Hall

Originally opened in 1950 as the home of Humboldt’s Industrial Arts program, the building was named for Horace “Pop” Jenkins, a ceramicist and faculty member who championed hands-on learning.

Renovation

A Modern Home

Following a $12 million renovation funded by the California State University’s Chancellor’s Office, Jenkins Hall re-opened in Fall 2025 as a modern home for the Sculpture and Ceramics programs.

Studios

Concept to Finished Work

The renovated facility now features studios, fabrication spaces, kiln rooms, and a 3D printing room where students can work with wood, metal, or clay, continuing Jenkins’ legacy of craftsmanship. The complementary facilities allow students to move seamlessly from concept to finished work.

The Foundry

Metal Casting

One of the building's most distinctive spaces is The Foundry. Here, students learn the art of metal casting—pouring molten metal into molds and transforming them into sculptures—an ancient process that remains central to contemporary sculpture.

First Floor

Sculpture Lab

On the first floor of Jenkins Hall is the Sculpture Lab. It includes areas for metalworking, woodworking, mold-making, mixed-media projects, and digital fabrication, so students can explore a wide range of artistic processes from concept to completion.

Second Floor

Ceramics Studio

The Ceramics Studio on the second floor of Jenkins Hall supports every stage of the ceramic-making process. The studio includes a variety of facilities for students to explore both wheel throwing and handbuilding, developing their work from raw clay to finished pieces.

Clay, Glaze, and Fire

Surface Design

The studio has dedicated spaces for clay preparation, glazing, firing, and mold-making. Low- and high-fire facilities allow students to learn a variety of firing techniques, while the glaze room serves as a place for experimentation with color, texture, and surface design. Together, these spaces create an environment where craftsmanship, creativity, and self-expression thrive.
